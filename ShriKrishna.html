The Destroyer within the Trimurti


The Stealing of Butter

As a child, Krishna was notorious for his love of butter and would often steal it from the houses of the villagers. One day, Krishna and his friends devised a plan to reach a pot of butter hanging high from the ceiling. Forming a human pyramid, Krishna climbed to the top and gleefully helped himself to the butter. Despite being caught by the village women, Krishna's charm and innocent mischief endeared him to everyone. His playful antics, often seen as divine leelas (pastimes), symbolized his connection with the common people and his ability to bring joy and laughter into their lives, even in the simplest of acts.

The Lifting of Govardhan Hill

When Krishna was a young boy in the village of Vrindavan, the villagers prepared to worship Indra, the rain god, to ensure timely rains. However, Krishna persuaded them to worship Govardhan Hill instead, arguing that it provided them with all necessities. Enraged by this, Indra sent torrential rains to flood the village. To protect the villagers and their cattle, Krishna lifted Govardhan Hill with his little finger, providing shelter beneath it. For seven days, Krishna held up the hill, demonstrating his divine power and humbling Indra. The villagers were in awe of Krishna's might and devotion, recognizing him as their protector and guide.

The Defeat of Kaliya

In his youth, Krishna learned of a poisonous serpent named Kaliya, who had contaminated the waters of the Yamuna River, causing distress to the people of Vrindavan. Determined to rid the river of this menace, Krishna dove into the waters and confronted Kaliya. A fierce battle ensued, with Krishna dancing on the serpent's multiple heads, subduing him with his divine strength and agility. The serpent's wives pleaded for mercy, and Krishna spared Kaliya, instructing him to leave the river and never return. This act not only cleansed the Yamuna but also reinforced Krishna's role as a divine protector and benefactor of his people.
